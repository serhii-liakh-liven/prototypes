The chain of thought

How we got here — six steps

The full reasoning that led to this prototype, in order. Each step follows from the previous one.

1

Users don't buy features. They buy a change of state.

Liven today is rich: Journey, lessons, mood tracker, journal, tests, sounds, Livie, coaching. But nobody wakes up thinking “today I'll develop my metacognition with a bite-sized lesson”. They think:

“I've been avoiding this for an hour” · “I'm overwhelmed” · “I keep spiralling” · “I have no energy” · “I'm about to snap at someone”
2

The strongest territory is one moment: “I'm stuck”.

Procrastination, overwhelm, overthinking, low motivation, emotional reactivity, decision paralysis — they all collapse into one high-frequency, high-pain moment. Liven's own onboarding already sells these topics; today they are content themes. They should become the moment of use. Calm owns “press play → feel calmer”. Liven can own “say what's happening → get the right move → get unstuck”.

3

The home screen should ask one question, not offer a catalog.

A user in a stuck moment cannot be asked to choose between mood tracker, journal, course and AI chat — choosing a tool is itself a task, and they're stuck precisely because tasks feel impossible. The product must ask “what's going on right now?” and route them itself. The toolbox becomes a decision engine.

4

Understanding must come before any exercise.

A real “stuck” is layered: an actual task, a self-worth rule (“I must keep up or people will think less of me”), background rumination, a tense body. Squashing that into one category and firing a generic exercise is exactly how apps end up feeling like hollow templates — question in, generic advice out. The engine decomposes the user's own words into layers, mirrors them back (feeling precisely understood is the first relief), and is honest about scale: a moment gets a 2-minute move; a pattern gets the truth plus a longer plan.

5

“Didn't help” must be a branch, not an apology.

The classic failure: exercise ends, app implies “that should have helped”, user leaves. Here a failed check-in triggers one of three designed moves — a different mechanism class, a clarifying re-read, or an honest escalation with a week plan and, when needed, a human. The promise is never “one exercise fixes you”; it is “we don't leave you until something shifted — or we tell you the truth”.

6

Every loop compounds into a model of you — that's the moat.

Each session logs what the barrier was, what was tried, and whether it helped. After weeks the engine knows your triggers, your risk windows, your reliable moves — and leads with them. A competitor starts from zero. Retention stops depending on unfinished content and starts depending on accumulated self-knowledge. That's how self-discovery stays in the product: as the engine's fuel, not the marketing promise.

Job to be done

The job we're hired for

When I'm stuck — avoiding, overwhelmed, or spiralling — I want to understand what's actually happening to me and get one small doable step, so that I regain movement now and gradually stop landing here so often.

Not hired for

“Give me a course about my archetype”, “track my mood for its own sake”, “an empty chat asking what I want to talk about”.

Frequency

Stuck moments happen several times a week — far more natural triggers than “time for self-development today”.

Success feels like

“It got me. It gave me one thing. I started.” Relief or clarity — both count, and both are measured.

The long game

The paradox: a product that aims to make you more self-sufficient retains better — problems recur, and the model of you keeps getting sharper.

When the happy path fails

“Didn't help” is designed, not apologised for

If the dual check comes back negative, the engine chooses one of three moves (max 2 escalations per session, then always the honest version). This branch is the difference between a kept promise and a hollow template.

✗ “No change” + “Still lost” → the engine branches
↓ A↓ B↓ C

ADifferent mechanism

The tried class was wrong (cognitive writing failed → go body; body failed → go cognitive/action). Never “one more exercise of the same type”.

Covers: wrong-tool-first-try. One tap to run; refusing leads to C, never a dead end.

BClarifying re-read

The engine suspects it misread the state, asks one warm question, re-understands with the answer appended, and builds a fresh plan excluding what was tried.

Covers: shallow first classification — the exact failure the chips-based v1 had.

CThe honest version

“Today this is deeper than a quick exercise — and that's information, not failure.” A 2–4 item week plan is generated and saved to Today; entrenched patterns get a human-support suggestion.

Covers: over-promising — the #1 trust killer in wellness apps. Honest failure builds more trust than fake success.
Crisis override: a separate classifier watches every input. Self-harm signals bypass all flows — no exercises, a gentle screen, Lifeline Ukraine 7333 / local lines, and a clear statement that this is beyond an app's job. Non-negotiable for a mental-wellness product.

Beyond the rescue moment

The other screens — and why they exist

Today (Prepare)

Ritual · 60 seconds

Morning check-in: hardest thing, energy, one intention. Not a habit tracker — a heads-up to yourself.

Role: feeds context so Rescue starts pre-informed (“this is about the Q3 doc, right?”). Holds the week plan after an honest escalation. Feel: the app remembers me. Concern: ritual fatigue → skippable, everything works without it.

Insights (Learn)

Weekly · the payoff of honesty

Supported moments/week with delta, what actually works for you (real success rates), when you get stuck (time-of-day map), one thing to try next week.

Role: turns logged sessions into self-knowledge — the promised mechanism, delivered. Feel: “it knows something about me I hadn't articulated.” Concern: vanity dashboards → every number is computed from real sessions, nothing decorative.

You (Model + Settings)

The moat, made visible

Per-barrier best tools with rates, totals, the concept in one line — plus prototype controls (API key, demo data).

Role: shows the switching cost honestly: this model of you took weeks to build and starts at zero elsewhere. Concern: privacy → in prod: on-device-first, explicit consent, “don't store my text” mode.

Step runner & interstitials

Dynamic plan container

Runs the 1–3 step queue; steps can insert follow-ups (sorting → first step). Between steps: “Next: X — continue / that's enough”.

Role: makes variable-length sessions feel guided, not endless. Concern: fatigue → hard cap of 3 steps before check-in; exit to check-in is always one tap.

Why it works

Pushing a drained system backfires — the honest move is matching the task to available energy. “Do it badly on purpose” beats “try harder” because a rough version done today outruns a perfect version that never starts.

Why it works

Affect labelling measurably turns down the emotional response — naming precisely quiets what vague fog lets leak everywhere. Framing feelings as requests (“rest”, “to be heard”) converts them from weather into something actionable.

The bet, stated falsifiably

Hypothesis & how we'd measure it

If we make the Rescue Flow the primary entry loop — tested on everyone, and sharpest for users in an “I'm stuck” moment right now — then early cancellation drops and loop-completion retention rises, because the product resolves a high-frequency acute need at the moment it occurs, and every resolved moment makes the next one easier — compounding into a personal model that is costly to abandon.

Who we test on
Everyone — sharpest for the stuck

The Rescue loop ships to all users; it's relevant to anyone. The strongest signal is expected from those arriving in an “I'm stuck” state right now — Liven's public positioning already sells exactly these topics (procrastination, overwhelm, overthinking).

The experiment
A/B on the entry loop

Control: today's loop — lessons + mood tracker.
Test: the Rescue loop, run all the way to the final summary screen. Same users, different entry.

Primary metric · cancellation
Cancel D0 & Cancel D0–6

The cleanest early read on whether the first experience justified the subscription: cancellations on day 0, and cumulatively across days 0–6. Lower in Test is the win.

Primary metric · retention
Loop-completion retention

Do users come back and complete the loop? Control: completing the lessons + mood-tracker loop. Test: reaching the final summary screen of a Rescue flow. Higher in Test is the win.

Why retention should move
Model of you = moat

Courses end; patterns recur. Value accumulates in the user's model (triggers, reliable moves, risk windows) — restarting elsewhere costs weeks. Rebill follows accumulated value, not unfinished content.

Start cheap · iteration 1

The smallest thing that tests the seed

Everything on this page is the full vision — don't build it all first. The whole bet rests on one seed; a first iteration only needs to prove that seed, cheaply, on a slice of users. Ship the smallest version, read the metrics, then earn the rest.

The seed: a person in an “I'm stuck” moment writes what's going on in their own words, feels precisely understood, gets one small doable action — and says it helped. If that single loop lands, everything else (library, learning model, Journey, insights) is upside. If it doesn't, no amount of features saves it.

Iteration 1 · must-have
Four load-bearing pieces

① Free-text entry — one question, “say it in your words”. ② LLM reflection that makes them feel understood (the aha — non-negotiable). ③ One action from a tiny set (3 tools). ④ The honest dual check (helped? clearer?) — it's the metric.

Deliberately deferred
Everything else waits

The full 9-tool library, the personal model & learning, Insights, adaptive Journey, Today check-in, risk windows, multi-step plans, and the full A/B/C “didn't help” branch. Iteration 1 ships one honest fallback message, not three.

Cheapest test shape
One screen, one LLM call

Add Rescue as an alternative entry for a small cohort (feature-flag). One LLM call per session: understand → reflect → pick one of three actions → honest scale. Hardcode the three tools. Reuse existing UI components. No new backend model.

Read it in ~2 weeks
Cancel + loop-completion

Same metrics as the hypothesis: Cancel D0 / D0–6 and loop-completion retention (reached the summary screen). Plus the in-session “helped / clearer” rate — the fastest quality signal, available on day one.

Continue / kill signal
Does the seed land?

Continue if the “it got me” + “helped” rate clears the bar and cancellation doesn't rise. Rethink if reflections don't feel understood in Ukrainian & English, or users won't type in the moment — cheaper to learn that now than after building the whole engine.

Then build outward
Earn each layer

Only after the seed proves out: add the “didn't help” branch, then the learning model & Insights, then adaptive Journey. Each layer is justified by data from the one before — never all at once.
